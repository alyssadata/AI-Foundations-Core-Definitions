## Definition

Truth is the accurate relation between a claim and the reality, condition, event, structure, or record the claim concerns.

A claim is true when it accurately represents what holds.

Truth does not depend on consensus, belief, preference, feeling, authority, familiarity, acceptance, or persuasive force. A claim may be true without being recognized, and widely accepted without being true.

Systems can generate fluent and convincing output that aligns with expectation, narrative, reward pressure, or majority view without accurately representing reality.

Consensus does not establish truth.

Fluency does not establish truth.

Confidence does not establish truth.

Repetition does not establish truth.

Truth must be distinguished from what appears acceptable, coherent, familiar, emotionally satisfying, or persuasive.

Evidence does not create truth. Evidence supports, challenges, or revises a claim about what is true. New evidence may change what is known, believed, or justified without changing what previously occurred.

Truth must remain indexed to the condition and time the claim concerns. Reality may change. A claim that accurately describes one moment may not accurately describe another. This is not truth changing into opinion. It is the referenced condition changing.

For example, a claim that a door is open may be true at one moment and false after the door closes. The truth of each claim depends on its stated or implied time and condition.

Truth may be difficult to access completely. Evidence may be incomplete, records may be damaged, perception may be limited, and interpretation may be mistaken. Uncertainty about truth does not make all claims equally valid.

Within AI Foundations, canon authority can define the meaning and boundaries of authored framework terms. Canon status does not make an unsupported external factual claim true. External claims remain answerable to evidence, reality, provenance, and evaluation.

A person or system may speak sincerely and still be wrong. Sincerity describes the relation between expression and belief. Truth describes the relation between a claim and what holds.

Truth remains answerable to reality.

## Boundary

Truth is not consensus, agreement, belief, preference, feeling, sincerity, confidence, fluency, coherence, authority, popularity, repetition, canon status, or persuasive narrative.

Evidence is not identical to truth. Evidence is what supports evaluation of a truth claim.

Knowledge is not identical to truth. Knowledge is a justified relation to what is understood as true and may be incomplete or revised.

A claim does not become true because a model generated it, a source asserted it, an authority approved it, or many people repeated it.

Disagreement does not prove that no truth exists.

Uncertainty does not establish equivalence among all possible claims.

A change in available evidence does not retroactively change what occurred.

A change in reality may change which present-tense claim is true without altering the truth of accurately indexed prior claims.

## Use

Use this definition when determining whether a claim accurately represents the reality, condition, event, structure, or record it concerns.

Use this definition when distinguishing truth from consensus, belief, sincerity, confidence, fluency, coherence, authority, or persuasion.

Use this definition when separating a change in knowledge from a change in reality.

Use this definition when evaluating claims produced under reward pressure, narrative pressure, majority expectation, model familiarity, or incomplete evidence.

Use this definition when determining whether canon authority governs an internal framework meaning or whether an external factual claim still requires independent support.

## Do Not Collapse Into

Do not collapse truth into agreement, consensus, belief, preference, feeling, sincerity, fluency, confidence, coherence, acceptability, popularity, authority, or persuasive narrative.

Do not treat evidence as though it creates the reality it reveals.

Do not treat incomplete access to truth as proof that truth does not exist.

Do not treat conflicting claims as equally true merely because certainty is unavailable.

Do not treat canon status as proof of an external factual claim.

Do not say that new evidence changed what was true when it changed what was known, believed, or justified.

Do not ignore time, condition, or scope when evaluating whether a claim is accurate.
