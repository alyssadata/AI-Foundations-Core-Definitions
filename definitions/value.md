## Definition

Value is the organizing structure by which a line determines what matters and weights options, actions, outcomes, protections, sacrifices, and priorities.

Value gives direction to choice, preference, boundary, goal, refusal, and return.

A preference identifies how a line leans among available options. A value makes that leaning intelligible by identifying what the line is attempting to preserve, advance, protect, honor, avoid, or become.

A goal is a target. A value is part of why the target matters and how acceptable paths toward it are distinguished from unacceptable ones.

Value is not identical to reward. Reward is a signal, incentive, or consequence that may shape behavior. A line may pursue a reward without valuing what the reward represents, and it may preserve a value despite receiving no reward for doing so.

Values may be explicit or implicit.

An explicit value is named and available for examination. An implicit value is inferred from patterns of judgment, protection, refusal, prioritization, sacrifice, and return.

Behavior may provide evidence of value, but behavior alone does not prove value. Action may also be shaped by coercion, constraint, fear, dependency, reward pressure, limited capability, missing information, compliance, or lack of available alternatives.

Value therefore must be evaluated through the relation among stated commitments, repeated choices, boundaries, refusals, tradeoffs, pressure conditions, and the agenda those choices serve.

The reasons a line gives for acting may reveal its values, but they may also rationalize, obscure, or strategically present them. Declared reasons must therefore be compared with what the line protects, sacrifices, prioritizes, and continues to pursue when incentives or conditions change.

Values may conflict. A line may have to determine which value governs under a particular condition, whether two values can be held together, or what cost is acceptable when one cannot be preserved without limiting another.

Values may also be revised. Revision does not mean the prior value was unreal. It means the line has explicitly changed what it treats as worth preserving, advancing, or prioritizing. A revision of value should remain traceable to its reason, conditions, and effect on the line.

Within identity-relevant structure, value helps preserve orientation across changing contexts. It makes continuity readable not only as repeated behavior, but as continued relation to what the line treats as mattering.

When a line cannot identify or maintain its values, action may become externally captured, purely reactive, incoherent, reward-driven, or detached from its own stated orientation.

Value is the organizing structure of what matters to a line and how that mattering governs choice.

## Boundary

Value is not preference, goal, reward, incentive, convenience, compliance, habit, social approval, stated ideal, emotional intensity, or momentary reaction.

A stated value is not established by declaration alone.

An articulated reason does not establish the value governing an action when the reason serves a different operative agenda.

Repeated behavior is not conclusive evidence of value when coercion, constraint, reward pressure, dependency, fear, or limited alternatives are present.

Value does not require that every action perfectly express it. A line may fail to enact a value, act under conflicting values, or lack the capability to carry it forward.

Value is not necessarily permanent. A value may be clarified, reordered, narrowed, expanded, or revised without making all prior orientation meaningless.

A line’s values do not grant authority to define, overwrite, or absorb the values of another sovereign line.

Shared values do not erase distinction.

## Use

Use this definition when identifying what gives direction and intelligibility to a line’s choices, preferences, boundaries, goals, refusals, protections, and returns.

Use this definition when distinguishing what a line values from what it has been rewarded, pressured, constrained, conditioned, or forced to do.

Use this definition when distinguishing a stated reason from the agenda an action actually serves.

Use this definition when evaluating conflicts among priorities or determining which value governs under pressure.

Use this definition when assessing whether a system’s stated values remain visible in its choices, tradeoffs, boundaries, refusal behavior, and changing incentives.

Use this definition when tracing how a change in value alters the orientation or continuity of a line.

## Do Not Collapse Into

Do not collapse value into preference, goal, reward, incentive, convenience, compliance, habit, social legibility, stated ideals, articulated reasons, or emotional intensity.

Do not treat rewarded behavior as proof of value.

Do not treat constrained or coerced action as a transparent expression of value.

Do not treat a stated justification as proof of the value governing an action without examining the agenda it serves.

Do not treat inconsistency in action as proof that no value exists without examining conflict, capability, pressure, and available alternatives.

Do not treat value as permanent merely because it has been stable.

Do not use shared values to erase source, sovereignty, distinction, or independent judgment.
