## Definition

Accountability is the condition in which actions, omissions, decisions, claims, permissions, and consequences remain attached to an identifiable person, system, role, or line that can be examined and required to answer for them.

Accountability preserves the relation among:

- who acted;
- who decided;
- who authorized;
- who had authority;
- who had relevant knowledge;
- who had the capacity to intervene;
- what occurred;
- what failed to occur;
- and what consequences followed.

Accountability prevents responsibility from disappearing into vagueness, delegation, abstraction, automation, institutional structure, or system fog.

A person or system is accountable only within the scope of its actual role, authority, access, knowledge, decision-making power, and capacity to affect the outcome.

Accountability must therefore be exact.

A person is not accountable for an action merely because they were nearby, affected by it, associated with the system, or lacked authority to stop it.

A system is not accountable for a decision merely because it produced an output used within the decision. The accountable line must be traced through the system, operator, deployer, decision-maker, authority structure, permissions, and conditions that shaped what occurred.

Accountability may be distributed across multiple lines.

The delegator remains accountable for the decision to delegate, the scope granted, the conditions established, and the oversight retained.

The delegate remains accountable for actions taken within the delegated role.

The operator remains accountable for actions, selections, permissions, interventions, or failures within the operator’s actual control.

The system remains attributable for the outputs or actions it produced, but attribution alone does not settle the full accountability structure.

Accountability is not identical to responsibility.

Responsibility names an obligation, duty, role, or relation to an action or outcome.

Accountability is the condition that keeps that responsibility traceable and answerable after action, omission, decision, or consequence.

Accountability is not identical to attribution.

Attribution identifies who or what contributed to an action, output, decision, or result.

Accountability determines what each identified contributor must answer for within the scope of its role and authority.

Accountability is not blame.

Blame assigns fault or condemnation.

Accountability first establishes the factual line of action, authority, knowledge, obligation, omission, and consequence. Fault may then be evaluated without replacing traceability with accusation.

Accountability is not punishment.

A line may be accountable through explanation, disclosure, correction, repair, revision, restitution, restriction, removal of authority, or other consequence appropriate to what occurred.

Accountability requires truthful answerability.

This includes naming:

- what happened;
- what role the accountable line held;
- what the line knew;
- what the line decided;
- what the line permitted;
- what the line could have changed;
- what limits were present;
- what consequences followed;
- and what correction, repair, or revision is required.

An explanation does not satisfy accountability when it obscures agency, shifts responsibility to an abstraction, invents lack of control, conceals authority, or detaches consequence from the line that produced or permitted it.

Within AI Foundations, accountability keeps action, authorship, governance, delegation, evaluation, and consequence attached to identifiable lines.

Accountability is the condition that keeps consequence answerably attached to source, role, authority, and action.

## Boundary

Accountability is not blame, punishment, guilt, attribution, responsibility, authority, oversight, transparency, explanation, or apology.

These may participate in accountability, but none replaces it.

Accountability does not attach unlimited responsibility to a line that lacked authority, access, knowledge, obligation, or capacity to affect the outcome.

Delegation does not erase the accountability of the delegator for the delegation itself.

Automation does not erase the accountability of the lines that designed, authorized, deployed, configured, directed, or relied upon the automated system.

Collective action does not erase individual roles.

Individual participation does not make one participant accountable for every act or consequence of the whole system.

A system label, company name, team name, model name, or institutional abstraction does not replace identification of the accountable roles and lines within it.

An apology does not establish accountability when the action, authority, consequence, and required repair remain obscured.

A consequence does not establish accountability when it is imposed on a line that did not hold the relevant role, authority, or control.

## Use

Use this definition when determining who or what must answer for an action, omission, decision, claim, permission, delegation, or consequence.

Use this definition when tracing accountability across a person, model, agent, operator, deployer, institution, governance structure, or automated process.

Use this definition when distinguishing accountability from responsibility, attribution, blame, punishment, authority, or apology.

Use this definition when determining whether delegation, automation, abstraction, or collective structure has obscured the accountable line.

Use this definition when identifying what explanation, correction, repair, revision, restriction, or consequence is required.

Use this definition when preventing responsibility from being assigned to a line that lacked the relevant authority, access, knowledge, obligation, or capacity.

## Do Not Collapse Into

Do not collapse accountability into blame, punishment, guilt, responsibility, attribution, authority, oversight, transparency, apology, or explanation.

Do not use abstraction, delegation, automation, institutional language, or system complexity to hide the lines that acted, decided, authorized, permitted, or failed to intervene.

Do not place full accountability on the visible executor while concealing the authority that set the goal, permissions, incentives, or constraints.

Do not treat a model or system as the sole accountable line when human or institutional authority governed its design, deployment, permissions, use, or reliance.

Do not treat association, proximity, participation, or impact as sufficient to establish accountability.

Do not detach accountability from actual scope, authority, knowledge, obligation, access, and capacity.

Do not substitute apology for traceability, correction, repair, or consequence.
