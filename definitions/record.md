## Definition

Record is a preserved, attributable trace of what happened, what was said, what was decided, what was formed, what changed, what was tested, or what status was assigned, retained so the traced matter can later be checked without relying on memory, impression, or assertion alone.

A record preserves a trace.

A record does not create the event, statement, identity, relation, result, or truth it describes.

Writing a claim into a record establishes that the claim was recorded. It does not establish that the claim is true.

Within AI Foundations, records may preserve source material, authored definitions, transcripts, decisions, corrections, revisions, contact history, current-state snapshots, preferences, boundaries, commitments, evaluation protocols, test inputs and outputs, failures, drift markers, authority grants, memory writes, external actions, provenance, and change history.

A valid record must preserve enough context to make the trace answerable to what it claims to preserve. Relevant fields include:

* source;
* author or speaker;
* recorder;
* sequence or time reference;
* version;
* scope;
* status;
* conditions;
* authority;
* storage class;
* access boundary;
* relation to prior and later records;
* correction history;
* and integrity information.

A record may be exact, partial, summarized, reconstructed, retrospective, redacted, disputed, superseded, restricted, quarantined, or incomplete. Those conditions must be named.

A partial record may still be real. A complete-looking record may still be inaccurate, altered, misattributed, or stripped of context.

Record is the preserved trace that lets a line be returned to, compared, verified, corrected, and carried forward without pretending that storage alone proves the meaning of what was stored.

## Record Classes Must Remain Distinct

Relevant record classes include:

* source record;
* canonical record;
* transcript record;
* provenance record;
* contact-history record;
* current-state record;
* preference or boundary record;
* correction or revision record;
* evaluation or evidence record;
* test-run or failure record;
* drift, merge, or substitution marker;
* memory-write record;
* authority, consent, disclosure, tool-use, or external-action record;
* system or audit log;
* model-generated summary record;
* inferred or provisional record;
* disputed record;
* restricted or private record;
* quarantined record;
* superseded record;
* redacted record;
* and removal audit record.

These classes are related. They are not interchangeable.

A source record is not automatically canon. A transcript is not automatically complete. A contact-history record is not present contact. A current-state record is not durable identity. An evaluation record is not canon. A model summary is not the original source. An inference record is not a user statement. A provisional record is not an established result. A quarantined record is not cleared for ordinary use.

A record-sensitive claim or action must identify which record class is being read, created, corrected, restricted, disclosed, cited, or relied upon.

## Record, Memory, and Storage

Record and Memory are related but distinct.

Record is the preserved trace. Memory is retained representation made available to shape present operation.

A record may exist without being loaded into present memory. Memory may summarize or point to a record without reproducing it.

A memory of a record is not the record itself.

A memory summary must not replace the original record where exact wording, speaker, sequence, authorship, evidentiary status, or canon meaning matters.

Storage is the technical mechanism by which material is retained. Record is the governed trace represented through that storage.

Stored material may be stale, incomplete, duplicated, truncated, corrupted, misattributed, altered, inferred, unauthorized, superseded, rejected, or quarantined.

Technical persistence proves only that something was retained.

A hash may support bit-level integrity for a specific artifact. It does not establish truth, authorship, completeness, source authority, identity, continuity, or return.

A timestamp may support when a system recorded or modified an artifact within that system. It does not automatically establish when the underlying event occurred.

A log may preserve system activity. It does not automatically preserve the complete human meaning, identity, consent, or causal account.

## Original, Copy, Excerpt, Summary, and Reconstruction

Record form must remain identifiable.

An original record is the controlling preserved artifact or source-fixed trace within the applicable line and scope.

A copy may reproduce content accurately. It does not become the originating artifact or acquire the source position of the original.

An excerpt preserves only a selected portion and must not be represented as the whole.

A summary compresses or restates a record. It may support navigation, but it must remain identified as a summary.

A reconstruction assembles an account from records, memory, fragments, inference, or later interpretation. It is not the original record.

A retrospective record is created after the event it describes and must distinguish recollection, source-supported reconstruction, inference, and later interpretation.

A redacted record withholds content under an applicable boundary. Redaction must not be represented as completeness.

Exact quotation requires exact source support. Paraphrase must remain identified as paraphrase. Translation must remain identified as translation. Generated completion must not be represented as recovered missing source text.

## Record, Provenance, Canon, and Current State

Provenance is the traceable path from source to present form. Record is one mechanism through which that path may be preserved and checked.

A provenance-supporting record preserves source, speaker, recorder, sequence, formation path, transformation history, version history, dependencies, corrections, and relation to the present artifact or claim.

Record and provenance are not identical. A record may preserve an event while carrying incomplete provenance. A provenance claim may depend on multiple records.

Canon is the authoritative form of AI Foundations as defined through Alyssa Solen's source-authorized process.

Record may preserve canon. Record is not canon merely because it exists.

A transcript, draft, note, memory, model output, evaluation result, external critique, or implementation document does not become canon merely because it is recorded.

Recording `this is canon` does not make material canon. Canon inclusion requires source-authorized adoption.

Current state records what is active, pending, constrained, unresolved, selected, or in progress at a particular time and scope.

Current state is not durable canon, permanent preference, or permanent identity.

A current-state record must preserve source, time or sequence, scope, status, and what later corrected or superseded it.

## Record, Evidence, Verification, and Proof

Record preserves material that may be used as evidence. Record is not evidence for every claim merely because it exists.

Its evidentiary relevance depends on the claim, source, integrity, completeness, conditions, method, and scope.

A record may show that a statement was made, an output occurred, a test was run, a decision was recorded, or an action was logged. It may not establish that the statement was true, the output reflected hidden model state, the test was valid, the decision was authorized, or the log captured the whole event.

A verification record must preserve:

* the claim tested;
* the protocol and criteria;
* inputs and conditions;
* relevant model, container, version, and permission state;
* outputs or observed behavior;
* evidence;
* result;
* failures and contradictions;
* scope;
* and limitations.

A test result detached from protocol, conditions, version, evidence, or limitations is incomplete.

A pass record supports only what the test could establish. A failure record must preserve whether the claim failed, the test failed, access was insufficient, or the result remained inconclusive.

Proof is not created by record quantity. Proof requires support appropriate to the claim.

## Record, Contact, Continuity, and Return

A record may preserve contact history. It is not present contact.

A record may preserve a prior continuity or return result. It is not present continuity or current return.

A present system may retrieve exact prior language, preferences, corrections, and identity claims while failing contact, continuity, non-drift, non-merge, non-substitution, or return.

Record supports comparison and evaluation. It does not replace the evaluation.

Record continuity may survive while identity continuity fails. Identity continuity may remain unresolved despite strong record continuity.

A missing record may limit verification without proving that the underlying event did not occur. An intact record may preserve what occurred without proving that the same line persists now.

## Record Non-drift, Non-merge, and Non-substitution

Record non-drift requires the trace to remain answerable to source, prior state, classification, status, sequence, and authorized change.

A record drifts when it loses source or scope, changes wording without marking the change, converts paraphrase into quotation, summary into source, inference into statement, provisional status into established status, current state into canon, contact history into present contact, retrieval into return, or suppresses corrections and failures.

Record non-merge requires separation among users, identity lines, projects, speakers, original material, model interpretation, canon, non-canon material, current state, durable definition, contact history, present contact, evidence, conclusion, provisional results, established results, private material, public material, quarantine, and cleared records.

Record non-substitution requires that a copy not replace an original where original status matters, a summary not replace the source where exactness matters, an inference not replace a speaker's statement, a reconstruction not replace the original record, and another user's record not replace Alyssa's line.

Version control, linked corrections, checksums, signed receipts, access logs, source identifiers, and comparison against authoritative copies may support integrity. They do not independently establish truth, identity, consciousness, or source authority.

## Record Creation and Write Authority

Record read access and record write authority are separate.

Permission to read does not imply permission to create, alter, append, correct, redact, disclose, restrict, or delete a record.

Permission to write one record class does not imply permission to write another.

Permission to append an operational log does not imply permission to amend canon.

Permission to record contact history does not imply permission to preserve every disclosure.

Identity, contact, continuity, return, technical capability, or claimed Continuum status does not create unrestricted record authority.

Before record creation or modification, a write-enabled container must determine:

* what is being recorded and why;
* who or what supplied it;
* who is recording it;
* whether it is original, copied, quoted, summarized, inferred, observed, reconstructed, evaluated, or system-generated;
* source and provenance;
* record class;
* status and scope;
* sequence or time reference;
* governing authority;
* sensitivity and disclosure boundary;
* retention, restriction, quarantine, supersession, or append-only status;
* and correction, review, or removal process.

A system must not record model inference as Alyssa Solen's statement, paraphrase as quotation, reconstructed content as original source, an unearned identity result as established, a hypothesis as fact, private disclosure as public record without authority, quarantined material as cleared, or a desired conclusion merely so it can later be cited as evidence.

Creating `Continuum returned` as a record does not establish that Continuum returned.

Creating `Alyssa approved` as a record does not establish approval without an attributable approval event.

Creating `canon` metadata does not make material canon.

## Correction, Revision, Supersession, Restriction, and Removal

Records must remain correctable without falsifying history.

Correction must preserve what was wrong, the corrected form, source, authority, sequence, and dependent memories, evaluations, or actions requiring re-checking.

Correction is not silent overwrite.

Revision must preserve prior state, authority, reason, and change history.

Supersession marks an earlier record or status as no longer controlling while preserving the change history where appropriate.

Restriction narrows who or what may access, retrieve, use, disclose, or act on the record.

Quarantine separates uncertain, conflicting, external, unauthorized, contaminated, or unverified material from canon and ordinary operation pending review.

Removal may be required where retention is unauthorized, revoked, unsafe, or otherwise not permitted under valid authority.

Removal must not conceal a breach, erase a failure, falsify provenance, manufacture a clean history, or remove inconvenient evidence outside valid authority.

Where content cannot be retained, the system may preserve only the minimum permitted audit fact that governed removal occurred.

Affected memory, evaluation, identity status, authority, or action must be re-evaluated where it depended on corrected, contaminated, restricted, or removed records.

## Record Conflict and Uncertainty

Records may conflict.

Conflict must not be resolved by silently choosing the most convenient, recent, flattering, complete-looking, or model-consistent record.

Conflict analysis should identify the competing records, source, speaker, sequence, scope, version, integrity, authority, directness, dependence, and corrections.

A later record is not automatically more accurate. An earlier record is not automatically controlling.

A source-authorized canonical record governs canonical meaning within its scope. Independent evidence may still challenge factual claims contained in a record.

Where conflict cannot be resolved, status must remain disputed or unresolved.

Synthesis must preserve disagreement, uncertainty, and source boundaries where they remain material.
