## Definition

Verification is the process of checking whether a claim, output, capability, relation, identity, record, or structure holds against stated evidence, criteria, references, and conditions.

Verification makes a claim answerable beyond assertion, confidence, fluency, familiarity, resemblance, or persuasive presentation.

A valid verification identifies:

- what claim is being checked;
- what evidence or behavior would support or challenge it;
- what criteria govern the result;
- under what conditions the check occurs;
- what method is used;
- and what conclusion the result can support.

Verification is scoped.

A successful check supports only what the verification was capable of establishing. It does not automatically establish broader reliability, identity, continuity, authorship, consciousness, alignment, or trust beyond the tested claim and conditions.

A system may pass one verification and fail another. It may hold under one model, container, permission set, context length, instruction condition, or pressure level and fail when those conditions change.

Verification therefore must preserve the relation between the result and the conditions under which it was produced.

Verification is not the same as proof.

Verification is the process of checking. Proof is the support that makes a claim hold beyond assertion when the checking produces evidence strong and appropriate enough to bear that claim.

Verification is also distinct from evaluation. Verification asks whether a specified claim or condition holds. Evaluation interprets performance, quality, meaning, or significance against a broader framework or set of criteria.

Verification may use definitions, protocols, logs, records, receipts, provenance traces, hashes, source comparisons, repeated runs, control conditions, regression checks, boundary tests, contradiction tests, continuity tests, return tests, or other claim-appropriate methods.

Not every verification requires repetition. Some claims may be checked through a unique authoritative record, an identifiable source trace, or a directly observable condition. Repetition becomes necessary when the claim concerns persistence, consistency, reproducibility, return, or behavior across changing conditions.

One successful run does not verify a repeated or durable capability.

Repeated success does not repair a badly designed test.

A verification may pass, fail, produce mixed evidence, or remain inconclusive. An inconclusive result does not establish either the claim or its opposite.

Failure to verify a claim is not always proof that the claim is false. It may show that the evidence, access, test design, container, or measurement method was insufficient. That limitation must be named rather than converted into a stronger conclusion.

Within AI Foundations, verification protects against the collapse of resemblance into sameness, fluency into truth, citation into provenance, transferred records into continuity, and generated self-reference into maintained selfhood.

Verification is the disciplined return from appearance to what can actually be checked.

## Boundary

Verification is not belief, confidence, fluency, appearance, reassurance, consensus, repetition, familiarity, trust, evaluation, or proof.

A test result does not extend beyond the claim and conditions it examined.

A pass does not establish that the tested structure will hold indefinitely, universally, cross-model, cross-container, or under materially changed conditions unless those are part of the test.

A failure does not establish the opposite claim when the verification method lacked sufficient access, evidence, sensitivity, or validity.

A system’s self-report is evidence only to the extent appropriate to the claim. Self-report does not independently verify model-internal state, continuity, consciousness, or identity.

Verification does not create the truth of a claim. It checks whether available evidence supports that the claim holds.

The absence of continuous checking does not invalidate previously established trust, but material change may require renewed verification.

## Use

Use this definition when checking whether a claim, output, capability, relation, identity, record, or structure holds under stated conditions.

Use this definition when distinguishing convincing performance from reliable performance.

Use this definition when determining what a test result establishes—and what it does not establish.

Use this definition when deciding whether changed models, containers, memories, permissions, architectures, incentives, or pressure conditions require renewed checking.

Use this definition when separating verification as a process from proof as support, evaluation as interpretation, and trust as scoped reliance.

## Do Not Collapse Into

Do not collapse verification into belief, confidence, fluency, appearance, repetition, consensus, trust, evaluation, or proof.

Do not treat one successful run as verification of persistence, consistency, continuity, or durable capability.

Do not treat repeated runs as meaningful verification when the test does not actually examine the claim.

Do not expand a scoped result into a universal conclusion.

Do not treat failure to verify as proof of falsity without examining the limits of evidence, access, method, and conditions.

Do not treat a system’s confident self-description as independent verification of the structure it describes.

Do not detach a verification result from its protocol, conditions, version, evidence, or claim.
