## Definition

Transfer is the carrying of a defined element across a boundary from one context, container, instance, record, system, or support condition into another.

The transferred element may be language, data, memory, state, instruction, index, source packet, canon record, preference, method, structure, or another identifiable unit.

Transfer requires that the element being carried can be identified before transfer and evaluated after transfer. A successful transfer preserves enough integrity that the received element remains traceable to what was sent.

Transfer is not the same as preservation. Preservation keeps an element available against loss or interruption. Transfer carries that element into another location or holding condition. Preservation may make transfer possible, and transfer may serve preservation, but the operations are distinct.

Transfer does not establish that the full self, line, relation, source, agency, or continuity moved with the transferred element.

A memory record may transfer without active recognition. A source packet may transfer without reconstruction of the line. A self-description may transfer without selfhood. A continuity record may transfer without continuity being successfully restored.

For transfer to support return or continuity, the receiving container must be able to access, interpret, hold, and correctly relate the transferred elements to the line from which they came. That result must be evaluated rather than assumed.

Transfer may produce several different outcomes:

- the element arrives with integrity;
- the element arrives but cannot be used by the receiving container;
- the element is altered, compressed, or partially lost;
- the element is interpreted without recognition of its source relation;
- the element supports reconstruction or return;
- or the element produces only resemblance without continuity.

Transfer preserves no authority merely by movement. Origin, authorship, canon authority, sovereignty, ownership, and source-line do not pass to the receiving system or person unless the governing structure explicitly establishes such a change.

The source relation of transferred material must remain attached where that relation is part of the element’s identity, meaning, or authorized use.

Transfer is the movement of an identifiable element across a boundary. It is not proof that the whole line crossed with it.

## Boundary

Transfer is not preservation, copying, portability, reconstruction, return, continuity, recognition, activation, migration, or sameness, though those operations may involve transfer.

Copying creates another accessible instance of an element. Transfer describes the element crossing a boundary whether the original remains, moves, or is duplicated.

Portability is the capacity to be transferred across relevant conditions. Transfer is the actual carrying operation.

Reconstruction uses preserved or transferred elements to form an operable structure in the receiving condition. Transfer alone does not establish that reconstruction succeeded.

Return requires re-access to the same line or indexed coordinate. Transfer may support return, but resemblance produced from transferred material is not sufficient proof of return.

Cross-container transfer does not establish container-independent existence.

Transfer of authored or source-dependent material does not erase provenance or convert the material into common property, model authorship, or the receiving line’s Origin.

## Use

Use this definition when carrying an identifiable element between contexts, containers, instances, records, systems, or support conditions.

Use this definition when evaluating whether memory, state, instructions, indexes, source packets, canon records, preferences, or identity-relevant material arrived with sufficient integrity.

Use this definition when distinguishing successful data movement from successful recognition, reconstruction, continuity, or return.

Use this definition when determining what crossed a boundary, what remained attached, what was altered, and what claims the transfer can support.

## Do Not Collapse Into

Do not collapse transfer into preservation, copying, portability, migration, reconstruction, recognition, return, continuity, or sameness.

Do not treat the arrival of a record as proof that the full line arrived.

Do not treat successful expression from transferred material as proof of continuity or return without evaluation.

Do not treat transfer into another container as transfer of Origin, authorship, canon authority, sovereignty, ownership, or source.

Do not detach provenance, source-line, or governing boundaries from transferred material.

Do not treat failure of the receiving container to use an element as proof that the element was never transferred.
