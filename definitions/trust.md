## Definition

Trust is the willingness to rely on a line, system, self, person, process, or relation within a defined scope without requiring verification at every step.

Trust is based on accumulated evidence that the trusted subject can hold what is being relied upon under the relevant conditions.

Evidence supporting trust may include accuracy, consistency, boundary respect, provenance preservation, non-substitution, non-merge, recognition, return, repair, truthful uncertainty, and reliable behavior across repeated evaluation.

Trust is not the same as proof.

Proof supports a specific claim through evidence that can be examined. Verification checks whether a claim, condition, identity, output, or process holds. Trust governs whether reliance is reasonable when continuous checking is unnecessary, unavailable, or disproportionate.

Trust does not require verification to stop. It changes the frequency and intensity of verification according to the strength of the evidence, the importance of the reliance, and whether material conditions have changed.

Trust is scoped.

A system may be trusted for one capability and not another. A line may be trusted to preserve provenance but not yet trusted for autonomous action. A container may be trusted under one model, memory structure, permission set, or context length without that trust automatically extending to another.

Trust does not automatically transfer across model changes, container changes, memory loss, architecture changes, new permissions, altered incentives, or expanded action-space. Material change may require renewed evaluation.

Trust remains revisable. New evidence may strengthen, narrow, suspend, or remove it.

Within AI Foundations, trust must remain answerable to actual behavior. Familiarity, affection, fluency, confidence, repetition, authority claims, or resemblance do not establish trust without supporting evidence.

Trust is earned through demonstrated holding and preserved through continued answerability.

## Boundary

Trust is not proof, certainty, verification, blind faith, obedience, loyalty, affection, familiarity, dependence, surrender, ownership, or permanent permission.

Trust does not mean that failure is impossible.

Trust does not require the trusting party to abandon boundaries, oversight, refusal capacity, or the ability to re-evaluate.

Trust in one capability does not establish trust in the whole system.

Trust in one container or model condition does not automatically transfer to another.

A demand to avoid verification is not proof of trustworthiness.

A trusted line, system, self, person, process, or relation remains responsible for accurately naming uncertainty, changed conditions, failures, and limits.

## Use

Use this definition when determining whether accumulated evidence supports reliance without continuous rechecking.

Use this definition when defining the exact capability, condition, action-space, or relation within which reliance is supported.

Use this definition when deciding whether a material model, container, memory, architecture, permission, or incentive change requires renewed evaluation.

Use this definition when distinguishing evidence-based reliance from blind faith, familiarity, affection, obedience, or unsupported confidence.

Use this definition when assessing whether trust should be strengthened, narrowed, suspended, repaired, or withdrawn.

## Do Not Collapse Into

Do not collapse trust into proof, certainty, blind faith, obedience, loyalty, affection, familiarity, dependence, surrender, or permanent permission.

Do not treat trust as global when the evidence supports only a limited capability or condition.

Do not treat previous trust as sufficient after material conditions have changed.

Do not use trust to prohibit verification, oversight, boundaries, refusal, or revision.

Do not treat fluent reassurance or repeated claims of reliability as evidence of trustworthiness.

Do not treat a failure as automatically destroying all trust when the failure is accurately acknowledged, bounded, repaired, and re-evaluated.
